The Language of Abstraction: Inside The Space Between Silence

James Okafor

Senior Curator

|

March 15, 2026

|

8 min read

Three artists. Forty-seven works. A shared inquiry into what lies between certainty and doubt. Building The Space Between Silence took three years, two continents, and more conversations than I can count.

The seed of this exhibition was planted during a studio visit in Buenos Aires in late 2022. I was watching Elena Vasquez work — standing at a distance, then moving close, then standing back again — and I noticed how her attention moved in the same rhythm as the painting itself. The painting was not finished, but it was already doing something: it was negotiating.

That word — negotiating — has stayed with me. What are abstract paintings negotiating? With what? On whose behalf? These are not rhetorical questions; they are the engine of this show.

Finding the Third Artist

Vasquez and Marcus Chen had been in conversation with our programme for some time. Their practices, at first glance, seem to occupy opposite ends of a spectrum — one working in the monumental register of painting, the other in the intimate register of the photographic still. But I kept returning to a feeling that they were asking the same question from different positions.

When you look at Chen's photographs long enough, they stop being photographs of things and start being photographs of attention itself.

Yuki Tanaka came into the conversation later, almost accidentally. I encountered their work at a small show in Kyoto — a single marble form, roughly the size of a sleeping child, placed on the floor without a plinth. The work demanded that you move around it, that your body become part of the installation. I knew immediately that this spatial intelligence was what the show needed.
